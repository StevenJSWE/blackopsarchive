## Overview

The theory that elements of the Central Intelligence Agency were involved — directly or indirectly — in the assassination of President John F. Kennedy on November 22, 1963 in Dallas, Texas. Kennedy had been at war with CIA leadership since the Bay of Pigs disaster in 1961, had fired Director Allen Dulles, and was reportedly planning to fundamentally restructure the Agency.

## Key Evidence

**George Joannides:** A CIA officer later confirmed to have been case officer for the DRE (Cuban Student Directorate), a CIA-funded anti-Castro exile group. The DRE had documented confrontations with Lee Harvey Oswald in New Orleans in the months before the assassination. When the House Select Committee on Assassinations subpoenaed CIA records in the 1970s, Joannides was deployed as the CIA's liaison — without disclosing his prior operational connection to the DRE. This deliberate concealment was uncovered decades later by journalist Jefferson Morley after a lengthy FOIA legal battle.

**Withheld Documents:** As of 2024, thousands of JFK-related CIA documents remain partially or fully withheld from public release. Presidents Clinton, Bush, Obama, Trump, and Biden all deferred full disclosure despite a 1992 law mandating it.

**Oswald's Intelligence Connections:** Oswald defected to the Soviet Union in 1959 and returned to the United States in 1962 — with unusual ease at the height of the Cold War — accompanied by a Soviet wife. His movements and contacts in the months before the assassination included figures with documented CIA and FBI connections.

## House Select Committee Finding

In 1979, the House Select Committee on Assassinations concluded there was "probably a conspiracy" in Kennedy's death, based partly on acoustic evidence suggesting a second shooter on the grassy knoll. The National Academy of Sciences later contested the acoustic analysis. The committee could not identify who was responsible beyond Oswald.

## Status

**Alleged — not confirmed.** No direct evidence of CIA authorization of the assassination has been proven. However, documented CIA deception of congressional investigators, the concealment of the Joannides connection, and decades of withheld documents have prevented any definitive conclusion. The Warren Commission's lone-gunman finding has never achieved broad acceptance among historians or investigative journalists who have reviewed the primary record.
